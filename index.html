Punctuation Rules

1 Periods & Semicolons

  • Periods and semicolons are used interchangeably on the SAT and ACT
  • Both are used to separate two complete sentences
  • They can precede conjunctive adverbs (e.g., however, therefore) at the start of a clause
Incorrect: "Barbara McClintock...1983, she won the award."
Correct: "Barbara McClintock...1983. She won the award."

2 Colons & Dashes

  • Colons and dashes introduce lists and explanations and are treated the same on tests
  • They must follow a complete sentence that sets up the following information
  • They can be followed by either a full sentence or a fragment
Incorrect: "London is home to a variety of: museums, castles, and shops."
Correct: "Visitors to London often spend their time in three main types of attractions: museums, castles, and shops."

Comma Rules

3 Comma + FANBOYS

  • Comma + FANBOYS (for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so) is equivalent to a period or semicolon
  • Common conjunctions: and, but (so and yet are less common; or and nor are rarely tested)
  • Comma splices are incorrect
Incorrect (comma splice): "London is a very old city, it has some modern parts."
Correct alternatives: Use a period or semicolon: "London is a very old city; it has some modern parts."
When subject is implied: "London is a very old city but has many modern buildings."

4 Commas & Dependent Clauses

  • Dependent clauses (fragments) cannot stand alone as complete sentences
  • They begin with subordinating conjunctions (e.g., although, because, when, until, while, since, before, after)
Example: "Because London is a very old city, it has buildings from many different eras."
Rule: When a dependent clause precedes an independent clause, a comma must follow the dependent clause.
"Although London is a very old city, it has many modern buildings."

5 Transitional Words & Phrases

  • Transitional words/phrases test meaning; the correct choice creates logical meaning.
  • Types: Continuers (e.g., in addition, moreover), cause-and-effect (e.g., therefore, consequently), and contradictors (e.g., however, nevertheless).
Example: "The obvious reason for the construction of the Great Wall of China was protection. However, China had no powerful enemies when the Wall began to be built."

6 Non-Essential Information

  • Non-essential information can be removed without affecting the sentence meaning.
  • Punctuated with 2 commas, 2 dashes, or 2 parentheses; mixed punctuation is incorrect.
Example with commas: "London, which is a very old city, has some extremely modern parts."
Dashes: "London—which is a very old city—has some modern parts."
Parentheses: "London (which is a very old city) has some modern parts."

7 Commas with Names & Titles

  • Use commas around names/titles only if they are non-essential.
  • Essential names/titles: no commas (e.g., "engineering professor Vikram Iyer").
  • Non-essential names/titles: two commas, one before and one after (e.g., "a University of Washington engineering professor, Vikram Iyer.").
Incorrect: A single comma before a name/title in the middle of a sentence.

8 Additional Comma Uses & Misuses

  • Use commas to separate items in a list (e.g., "boost metabolism, aid weight loss, and increase endurance").
  • Use commas to separate adjectives whose order can be reversed (e.g., "active, warm-blooded animals").
  • Do not use commas before or after prepositions (e.g., "artists of the twentieth century").
  • Do not use commas between subjects and verbs (e.g., "The oldest surviving bridge in London is Richmond Bridge").
  • Do not use commas between compound items linked by "and" (e.g., "travel to London and Paris to visit museums and monuments").

Apostrophe Rules

9 Apostrophes (Plural vs. Possessive)

  • Apostrophes indicate possession: add apostrophe + -s for singular nouns (e.g., "cell's DNA").
  • For regular plural nouns, add -s or -es + apostrophe (e.g., "cells' DNA").
  • Irregular plural nouns: add apostrophe + -s (e.g., "people's responses").
  • Pronouns: possessive pronouns do not use apostrophes (e.g., "its" for possessive, "it's" for "it is").
  • Differentiate between "they're" (they are), "their" (possessive), and "there" (place).
  • "Who's" = "who is"; "whose" = possessive form of "who".

Pronoun & Noun Rules

10 Pronouns & Nouns

  • Pronouns replace nouns and must match in number: "he/she" for singular people, "it" for singular things, and "they" for plural.
  • Ensure pronouns correctly refer to their antecedents even if the antecedent is not in the same sentence.
  • Use "who" for people and "which" for things.
  • Use "whom" correctly: before verbs is incorrect; after prepositions is correct.
  • Nouns must agree in number, e.g., use "examples" for multiple items, not "example."

Verb Rules

11 Subject-Verb Agreement

  • Singular third-person verbs end in -s, while plural verbs do not.
  • Compound subjects connected by "and" require plural verbs.
  • Prepositional phrases and non-essential clauses between subjects and verbs do not change the verb's agreement.
  • Each and every are singular; collective nouns are treated as singular.
  • Be aware of subject-verb agreement in complex sentences and passive voice constructions.

12 Verb Tense

  • Maintain consistent verb tense throughout sentences and paragraphs.
  • Use the present perfect ("has/have been") for actions that started in the past and continue to the present.
  • Use the simple past ("published") for completed past actions.
  • Use the past perfect ("had studied") for actions completed before another past action.
  • Passive voice should be used cautiously to avoid wordiness and awkwardness.

Sentence Structure Rules

13 Parallel Structure & Word Pairs

  • Keep all items in a list parallel in structure (e.g., all nouns or all verbs).
  • For lists with only two items joined by "and" or "but," ensure parallelism; repeat infinitives as needed.
  • Certain word pairs must be used correctly and in parallel form (e.g., "either...or" and "not only...but also").
  • Ensure the constructions following pairs match in structure.
  • Maintain consistency in the use of word pairs and parallel structures throughout sentences.

14 Dangling Modifiers

  • Ensure the noun described by a modifier is immediately after the descriptive phrase to avoid dangling modifiers.
  • A dangling modifier occurs when the descriptive phrase does not clearly modify the intended noun.
Example of a dangling modifier: "Born in Mexico City in 1907, self-portraits were what Frida Kahlo was best known for."
Corrected version: "Born in Mexico City in 1907, Frida Kahlo was best known for her self-portraits."

15 Faulty Comparisons

  • Comparisons must be made between equivalent items: people with people and things with things.
  • Singular comparisons should compare the same type of singular items.
  • Plural comparisons should compare the same type of plural items.
  • Use "than" for comparisons, not "then."
  • Modify plural nouns with "number," "many," "fewer," and singular nouns with "amount," "much," "less."

16 Question Marks

  • Use question marks only for direct questions, which are separate from the main sentence.
Example of correct usage: "Most of the experiments performed by cognitive psychologist Elizabeth Spelke have been designed to test one main question: how much do babies and young children understand about the world around them?"
Example of incorrect usage: "Most of the experiments performed by cognitive psychologist Elizabeth Spelke have been designed to test how much babies and young children understand about the world around them?"
